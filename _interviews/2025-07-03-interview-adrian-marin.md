**Travis:** Okay, cool. So welcome to these casual, off-the-cuff conference organizing chats that I've been having. You are Adrian Marin, the organizer of Friendly.rb for the past couple years. I guess, to just start us off, I'd love to hear about your first conference, how that came about, maybe why you started it, and how it went.

**Adrian:** The first edition of Friendly? That's an interesting question. So that happened in 2023, and in 2022 I went, I think, to my first Ruby conference. It was Wroclove.rb in Poland. I went before to different web conferences and different technology conferences, but this was the first Ruby one, and I quite liked the vibe and the community, and everybody who put together that whole event and that whole experience.

Of course, I knew about RailsConf. I knew about EuRuKo, the big ones, but I didn't know a lot about the smaller conferences. Rails SaaS, I think the first edition was in 2022 as well, in September. And being 2022, 2023, there weren't as many conferences around because it was after the pandemic. So many of them shut down. We didn't know exactly if they'll ever come back or anything. So it was a little bit of a gray area.

<span id="starting-from-a-tweet" class="interview-anchor"></span>

I just tweeted, like, hey, if I run a conference in September in Bucharest, would anybody come? I got a lot of interest in that. A lot of people said, like, yeah, how much? When? Who's coming? Then I got to talk to more people. I spoke with Andy Croll and some other conference organizers to try to figure out, hey, is this feasible? Is this something that I could do on the side? Because I'm not an event organizer, and I don't have a team and everything.

It's not just me. I'm not the only host of Friendly.rb. I have four other friends, but we're not this big team. I don't have 50 employees that I can just throw on a conference. So I had a conversation with a few people. I had good, positive feedback on Twitter, and I said, like, what the hell, let's just do it. There aren't so many conferences around, so let's just try and make this a very memorable one. A small one, but very memorable.

**Travis:** How big was that first one? How many people came?

**Adrian:** One of the things that we wanted to do was make it as professional as I can, so as professional as I can without making it on an enterprise budget, because we don't have that. I knew that we won't have a lot of sponsors. I hoped, but we kind of knew nobody's going to sponsor the conference. Everybody told us, like, nobody's going to sponsor the conference. Maybe they'll sponsor you because they know you.

So we wanted to have a swanky, nice place, a nice venue. We did find an independent theater, which had a club nearby. It had very plushy seats, and it was very dark, and it has this nice wooden stage and everything. They had projectors and mics and everything you needed. And then the club was looking amazing, so that was the place where people can get out from the conference and go there. The only thing was they can only host 130, 140 people if they add another row. So 140, that's a hard stop for us.

**Travis:** Did you fill it up that first year?

<span id="selling-out-boutique" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Adrian:** Yeah, actually, we sold out. I think we sold 140, a little bit over 140 tickets every time. Not everybody showed up, but both editions, we sold more than, I think, 130 tickets.

**Travis:** Wow. That's pretty awesome. I feel like that's actually an anomaly in a lot of the people that I've talked to. When their first conference, if you don't already have a following, it can be hard to sell tickets that first time when nobody knows who you are. Did you have any sort of online presence before that? People kind of knew who you were, and so that's how you got more people to come?

<span id="touring-to-draw-crowd" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Adrian:** A little bit of an online presence, but nothing big. Even now on Twitter, I think I have 2,000 followers or something, and then maybe I had 1,000. So not a huge following.

But what I did, I went and toured a lot of the conferences in Europe. Most of them, I think. I was going to a conference every month, and I was meeting people. I spoke to a few conferences, maybe one or two, but I was going to them. Of course, as research as well. I wanted to see, what do people do? What do the Ruby people love at the conference, in an event?

And then I started talking to people, like, hey, I'm running a boutique conference. I told them, hey, if you want to come and hang out with cool, nice people in a nice city and learn something about Ruby in the fall, Friendly.rb is just for you. I think that's why maybe that was half of our success. Another good thing that happened is that a company from Spain had their company retreat in Bucharest at the conference. So that was about 25 tickets or something. That was just awesome. Whenever they filled in, just great to be able to host them.

But I think the gist was that I was going and talking about it. I was going to other conferences, and I was speaking about what I wanted to do. As a result, we only had maybe 20 people from Romania in our first edition. All the other people were international, from other places. That was a very big surprise for us. I think the name had something to do with it. It's friendly, Friendly.rb, and it really attracted only the friendliest people at this event.

**Travis:** That's cool. Do you know what percentage were from Europe versus from the Americas?

**Adrian:** I think a big percent, maybe 80%, were from Europe. And then we had people from the United States, Australia, Brazil, Kenya, all over the place. Whenever you bought a ticket, I think this still stands, whenever you buy a ticket for Friendly, we ask, where are you coming from? What country? And then we go on the website and add the flag somewhere in one of the sections. I said something like, hey, come and meet people from 30 countries or something. I think we ended up with 24 in the end.

That was awesome. I haven't seen that being made in other places and other conferences, but I thought that would be cool. I want to see who's coming. And we had people from all over.

**Travis:** That's really cool. Besides doing the tour and just talking to people, which obviously works really well, did you do any other marketing that first year? And has that changed over the years at all?

**Adrian:** Not that much. We did a little bit of guerrilla marketing. We posted on Twitter things like photos from Bucharest, because it is a very nice and beautiful city. We had beautiful photos that we'd post, like, hey, come. We're going to have this activity here.

Because this is another thing. We wanted to make it special. For example, between day one and day two, at the end of day one, I hired tour guides. We split people into groups, and we did walking tours. That was a great opportunity to get together a little bit and walk through the city and learn about the history, and learn about that place. We advertised for that.

Then on the third day, we advertised, like, hey, we're going to go to the mountains. On the third day, we're going to meet at the train station. We're going to get on a train two hours to the mountains. We're going to visit some castles and some mountains, and we're going to eat very traditional food. Then we're going to come back in the evening with the same train.

So that was a little bit self-serve. In the second edition, I think we charged for that so we can buy the tickets for everyone and stuff like that. We try to advertise the things that are going to make it special, the speakers and some other things. But it wasn't advertising advertising.

**Travis:** Sure.

**Adrian:** Paid ads in the paper.

**Travis:** Right. How did you get speakers that first year? Did you have a CFP or did you reach out to people?

**Adrian:** Oh, my God. That's a very good question. Right before we announced the actual conference, I reached out to a few very influential people from the community. A few were able to come back to us with a yes, and one of them was Xavier Noria. He was the keynote speaker at RailsConf the previous year, and he has a very heavy name in the community.

When I told him what we wanted to do, what kind of conference, what kind of event, and it's all for the community and everything, and we'd love him to come to the conference and speak, he said, like, yeah, sure, count on me. You figure out the details. I'll just come. That really gave us the trust. Okay, we can do this if we have Xavier here. That's going to be wonderful.

We opened with two speakers. Xavier, and another thing we wanted to do, this is something Andy Croll told me. I asked him at some point, do you want to come and speak at Friendly? He said, like, yeah, I'll come and speak, but only if it's not an all-white-male speaker lineup. Then everything clicked for me, like, oh, my God, you're absolutely right. It's so easy to make it an all-male, middle-aged, white lineup.

We were lucky enough to have very influential people from the community wanting to talk. I don't know why, because of the name or whatever. We had Jeremy Smith, we had Jason from Code with Jason podcast, and a few others. I'm blanking out on the names right now, but a few other influential people. We were like, oh, my God, what's happening? Everybody wants to come. Perfect. Let's do that.

We had a little bit of a half-and-half lineup in the first year. We had a few very famous people and some newcomers to the stage, because that was another thing that we wanted to do, to actually bring new people. My first speaking gig was a year previously, and I know how difficult that was. We are trying to bring new people to the stage and give them the microphone so they can start their careers, kick it off, and do their own things as well.

**Travis:** Did you do anything like honorariums? Did you help pay for the travel and stuff for the speakers? Because that can get very expensive, especially with people coming from across the ocean. How did you do that?

**Adrian:** What we did was, we knew that we're not going to have the big sponsors with us, and we also knew that respectable, pretty good conferences, or the ones that have the means, they pay for at least the stay, or for the travel, or for both, or whatever they can. We couldn't do that, because we didn't have the money. Second of all, for two days, that's twice as many speakers from one day, and the math doesn't really add up if you're not a big, big conference.

If you have one day, that's probably, what, six or eight speakers? That's different versus 12 or 16. I don't know how many, 14 I think we had in total. So we couldn't do that. But we said something else, like, okay, we're going to give everyone $300 or euros or something, and you can do whatever you want with it. It's going to be cash. That's what we can do.

At that point, I think RailsConf, for a big conference, was offering 500. I said, hey, if the big conference is doing 500, we can do 300. It's at least a good amount of money. It's not a lot of money, but in Romania you could pay the hotel, and maybe the flight as well in Europe, because of the low-cost flights and everything.

So we did that speaker honorarium. We had an amazing speaker dinner. This is another thing we had. In the first year, we went to a very nice traditional restaurant where, at some point, people come and do traditional dancing and everything, and amazing food, vegetarian and non-vegetarian. We wanted to have an amazing experience for the speakers. That was a great opportunity for us to get to know them a little bit better, for them to get to know each other better, and be more familiar and unwind for the whole thing. So speaker honorarium, the speaker dinner, and I think we gave them a small swag bag. It wasn't anything big, but it was a good, nice one.

**Travis:** Speaking of sponsors, did you have any that first year? And how were you able to get those?

<span id="simple-sponsorship-package" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Adrian:** That's a funny story. Again, you kind of wing it in the beginning. This is what we did. We went to different conferences and checked, what kind of sponsorship packages they offer? Booth, no booth, logos, big logo, five logos, five postings on Twitter or social, or whatever. At first, we had kind of those packages of, I don't know, 1,500, 2,500, 4,500. I think we kind of stole them from Blue Ridge Ruby, because they had an amazing display of them. It seemed that they thought it out, and it kind of seemed that we're a similar size conference.

For the first few months, nobody said yes. We tried to reach out to a lot of companies, from big Shopify and GitHub to smaller ones and everything. Nothing panned out. And then Lucian had this idea of like, hey, what if we do one package? I think it was $1,250 or something like that. We said, okay, it's called community. It's pretty affordable, and whoever wants to do it, let's do it. We're going to have only 10 spots available. Everybody's going to do the same.

We had five people, five companies slash people that wanted to get in on it. So it was Andy Croll, it was Evil Martians, it was Nick from Trailblazer, and I'm blanking out again, and a few other companies. One from Romania, and Flagrant from the US. You might know Flagrant. They just used a Stripe link, and that was amazing. In the morning I checked my Stripe account: somebody sent you some money. Like, oh, my God, why did you do that? We think you're doing a nice conference, a nice event, and we wanted to help out. And no, $1,000, that's not a lot. It worked. It really worked. So we had five sponsors of $1,200 for each one. We made it work. Let's call it that. We made it work.

**Travis:** Do you think that the change was just making it simpler? Made it easier for companies to say yes, because before they were just overwhelmed with choice, and they were just like, I don't want to think about it. Is that what changed?

**Adrian:** Maybe. Yeah, maybe that's one way of putting it. But it's also affordable. It's $1,000. If you're a well-enough-paid developer, you're probably going to make $4,000, $6,000, $8,000 per month, whatever, depending. So $1,000 is not that much. We kind of hoped, like how Andy did it, Evil Martians, it's very affordable. People and organizations that believe in this thing, believe in the community, are just going to be like, okay, $1,000, I want to pitch in. It's not that big of a deal.

When you have like, okay, it's 1,500, then it's 2,500, it's 4,000, and which one should I take? Making it simpler and affordable, that was, I think, why it worked.

**Travis:** Interesting. I just got my venue, and I'm thinking about sponsors now. I'm doing that thing right now where I'm like, what packages do I need to have? What do I name them? Ruby, gold, all that stuff. But I really like the idea of just making it simple. To be honest, companies don't really care what the name of the sponsor package is. A lot of them just want to support the community, or get their name out there, or be there, have a presence for hiring purposes.

Maybe just simplifying it, especially the first year, if you're doing a lot of the work as a single person or splitting up the work, I feel like that might be an interesting strategy to take. So I might steal that. That's pretty interesting.

**Adrian:** Yeah.

**Travis:** I haven't heard of anybody else doing that, so I like that.

**Adrian:** We actually sponsored, with Avo, it's called Tiny Rubiconf in Helsinki. They are the organizers of EuRuKo 2020, I think. They said the same thing, like, we're going to have two spots for $1,000 or $1,500 or whatever. Whenever I saw that, I said, okay, this is my time to pay it back. We want to sponsor, and whatever. It's not a big effort.

I think it's another thing to think about, like what kind of event are you planning? If it's a big one, like companies, for example, when they sponsor EuRuKo or RailsConf, usually they want to hire. That's a big pool of developers. You're going to get in front of a lot of eyes. Of course you're going to make a big deal. It's going to cost $10,000, $15,000, $25,000, whatever. You're going to have a booth and people there and training and everything.

When it's a small conference, we actually last year did have booths for two of our sponsors. It was nice, but they weren't at the booth every time. The booths were open, I think, in the first day, and then they mingled. Because with 100 people, you're going to know, you're not going to get to know everybody, maybe half of them. Or you're going to demo at the booth, and half of them you're going to talk with in the crowd.

So you got to figure out what's in it for them. What we say is, hey, do you want to have a friendly brand in you? Do you want to align your brand with a friendly brand? Some people feel that and say, okay, yeah, we want to do that. We see that you're doing amazing community work, and we want to help with that.

**Travis:** Nice. Love that. You mentioned earlier that you did a lot of research by touring all these conferences the year before you did your first Friendly. How was that? Did you learn anything? Did you steal any ideas from other conferences that you brought back for your first one?

**Adrian:** A ton of learning. That's a big learning experience, because you learn the things that you want to do, and you learn about the things that you don't want to do.

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Adrian:** I remember I went to conferences where people mumbled on the stage, or it took a long time to make the switch between the presenter and the actual speaker. I wrote that down in my mind, like, okay, we have to make that switch fast. Then I kind of saw that you got to have a place where people can evacuate the room and have some talks and whatever. I made a note of that. The drinks, maybe the food, maybe activities and everything. It's a big learning experience. It would be a lie to say I didn't steal some of those ideas.

**Travis:** Borrowed the ideas.

**Adrian:** Borrow the existing.

**Travis:** Of course, we're sharing.

**Adrian:** No, it's okay. We've been seeing some of our ideas pop up in the community. That's fine. That's all good, because it's all in the community.

**Travis:** It's all positive impact.

**Adrian:** Exactly. One of the things that I really did was I kind of enjoyed, I actually loved, the showmanship of Andy's. We're getting back to Andy Croll. Andy, if you see this. And I wanted to do the same thing. Be friendly and talk about—we talked about the code of conduct in the beginning, in the opening and everything. So a lot of that showmanship.

And it's not just a Ruby conference. So, for example, EuRuKo is a very technical conference. You go there. You're going to be in this auditorium in the computer science university. Everybody's doing hard, deep Ruby talks and discussions. That's a nice one, but I didn't want to do it like that. We had people that spoke about mental health, about the history of Roma people. We had dancers last year, traditional Romanian dancers. I wanted to make a show out of it. This is my opportunity to show people a good time and also learn. We have amazing speakers, but also show people a good time. So it was a different type of conference.

**Travis:** That's really cool. I did want to get into that, because I think that I want to do that as well. I like the technical conferences where it's just talks, one after another, for the whole time. You learn a lot, you get inspired by other people. But I think that something that makes conferences more memorable is the stuff that happens besides the talks. I heard that you did dancing. I heard, I don't know if it happened last year or if you're planning for hack spaces and pair programming. Can you talk about some of the things that you're doing besides talks?

**Adrian:** This year, Yaro came up with this idea. Last year and the previous year, me and Yaro, we go to a lot of conferences together, because he's an educator. He has this education channel, SuperRails, and education website with videos and everything. He talks to people about a lot of whatever he teaches in those videos. He does a lot of Hotwire Native now and Hotwire, and whatever. He did that unofficially at Friendly.rb and other conferences.

Now he said, hey, I want to do my SuperRails office hours, SuperRails hackspace, where people can come over and we can hack things together. Maybe Hotwire, Hotwire Native, whatever people want to do. We want to do something like that. I know that RailsConf did it, and RubyConf and Ruby Central does it, and that's amazing. That's a big production. We're going to be with Avo and some of our other open-source projects. We're going to be at this RailsConf and do the same thing.

But that's a big, big production. They invite everyone and they do everything. We can't do that. I don't want to do it perfectly. I said, I'm going to help you. I'm going to bring you a TV, and whatever. We're going to do it with Avo, because Paul is going to be there. So it's a low-hanging fruit. We're going to do that. If anybody else wants to do it, we're going to figure out something, because we're kind of open. We can do that. But it's not that big hackspaces kind of thing.

Otherwise, the dancing, that was a surprise. I wanted to do that. I wanted to do something special. We're going to have a few surprises this year as well. I don't want to spill the beans right now.

**Travis:** Oh, God.

**Adrian:** And a few surprises as well. But every year I try to do at least one thing interesting, because I don't know, I just feel that people that are coming back, they want to see some novelty as well, to get some value out of it.

**Travis:** Definitely. You remember the people that you meet, and you remember the things that happened. <span id="designing-for-connection" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Adrian:** If there's one thing that I want to tell you, and everybody that wants to do a conference, it's try to create the environment for people to get together and to open up. For us, one of the biggest ones was that we had long breaks, not a fixed schedule. That's another thing. We had the day-one activity, going throughout the city. A lot of people got to chat there. And the third-day activity, which was a full day in the mountains. Everybody told me, like, hey, oh, my God, I bonded with so many people, and we had this amazing opportunity that we couldn't have any other way.

When would I get to meet Jeremy Smith and talk to him about his family, and how we do things, and how he does things? You're never going to have that for a full day, right? So try to create these opportunities for people to get together and do all of that.

Jason did something at Sin City Ruby which was amazing. It was very Jason-y. He had a Ruby script with everybody's names, and he created groups of four or five people or six people, and he called it forced socialization. You got together, and he said, okay, for five minutes, 10 minutes, whatever, you're going to talk about something personal. It was a little bit forced, but whatever. It was Jason, it was Sin City, he can do whatever he wants.

That was a good thing. At first, it was a little bit cringe, but I found out a few amazing things about some people from the community which I wouldn't have been able to do. Because when you get to RailsConf, or a big conference, and you have this large hall, you're not going to go to a person like, hey, Travis, how's your family? What's up with you? You're not going to do that. It takes a while to get to that place. So try to think about creating these moments where people can get together and they actually can share from their lives.

**Travis:** I think that's another thing. Sin City, I think last year it was like 80 people or something. Some people are like, oh, I want to sell 200, 300, 500 tickets. But we actually need the regional conferences that are smaller because those are so much easier to meet people. It's so much less intimidating in a room of 80 people to just sit down next to somebody and say, hi, my name is Travis, versus RailsConf, where it's an auditorium of 1,000 people, and you walk in, everybody's already kind of in groups. Everybody's there with their company or whatever.

It's a lot harder to meet people. You have to try a lot harder, and you have to be a lot braver at those conferences. But at the small ones, that environment is nice. I think that's kind of the point of the small ones, like you said, making that open space for people to meet and just be social. So I love that. Definitely going to focus on that for the conferences that I hopefully put on.

Let's see what other questions I had here. I always ask everybody this because we get some interesting answers. Can you think of a small thing that you did that had an outsized or unexpected impact on the conference? Something that you just threw in there and you didn't even think about it, and then later people were like, I love that you did that thing, and you're like, wow, I almost didn't do that because I didn't think it was important.

**Adrian:** Yeah, there was one thing. It's the non-fixed schedule. What I do in the beginning of the day, I go through, like, hey, this is what we're going to do today. What I did, I didn't even put my mind to it. It just happened. I said, we're going to start at 10. We're going to have three talks, break at 12 or 1. We're going to have two hours of a break. We can go for lunch, and then another three hours of talks and breaks, but not scheduled. It's not like, okay, from 10 to 10:30, and then a break, and then from 10:45 to whatever. No, no, we'll figure that out.

What happened was, people went for very long breaks, 30 minutes. When I was coming into that hall to bring people in, like, okay, it's time for the next talk, come on, everybody was so into it. I felt a little bit bad getting into their conversations. Everyone was having so many conversations. That was a good thing. I remember Xavier came to me, I think, on the first day and said, oh, my God, that is an amazing idea that you did, without having a fixed schedule. Just leave it loose and everything. That's when it clicked with me, like, okay, yeah, that was a good idea. That was really nice.

**Travis:** That's cool. I haven't heard of that at other conferences either. I like that one. And definitely, Sin City Ruby, I think, kind of, I referenced that a lot, because I've only been to a couple of conferences, but that one was like that where he was pretty lax with the schedule, and he just kind of let things flow. I think people do appreciate that. Having those longer breaks too allows people to get into their conversations, whereas if you only have 15 minutes in between, sometimes people won't even start a conversation because they're just going to check their phone and their email really quick and maybe go to the bathroom. Then it's the next talk, and you don't have the opportunities to actually get people to say, well, I have 30 minutes, I might as well go start a conversation with somebody.

So that is really nice. Is there any piece of advice that you would give to anybody doing their first conference? Anything off the top of your head that maybe we haven't covered, or anything that you want to talk about that we haven't covered?

**Adrian:** It's a very open-ended question. The answer that comes to my mind is, try to be intentional with what you're trying to do. If you want to make a nice, regional, friendly, cozy conference, do that. If you want to do something bigger, okay, do that. But tell people about it. Be intentional.

At some point we were thinking, what happens if we sell more than 140 tickets? Would we have to find a bigger venue? We were asking the wrong questions. So try to be intentional about what you're doing, and try to do that thing in a nice way. If it's a regional small conference and you can do it on a lower budget, okay, do that one package of sponsorship. It's going to be easier for you to do that. Do the activities, because people are going to appreciate those. Also do the marketing a little bit.

Like I said, we didn't advertise. But actually, whenever, I think a month before, three months before, I got in front of a camera and said, hey, these are the updates. We have three new speakers, and we're going to do this, and we're going to have this surprise. Tell people about that, because that's how people are going to find out about the activities and everything else that you're planning to do. Be intentional about it and tell people about what you're going to do.

**Travis:** Definitely. Is there anything that you can give away, maybe for your upcoming Friendly this year, that you're excited about, or anything that you changed, that you're trying out?

**Adrian:** One thing that every year I'm excited about, and then once it ends, I'm afraid of what's going to happen: every year we have a very nice lineup for a conference of our size. We have speakers that you won't see in too many places, or you haven't seen them before. At the end of the conference, my question, every time, I'm afraid, like, what are we going to do next year? So the lineup. I'm very excited about this year's lineup.

One thing that we're going to do different, I think the mountain trip on the third day. We usually have 25, 30 people. Now we have 30 people, and we still have a few months until the event. So we're probably going to have more. We're probably going to rent a bus or something to do that. We'll figure it out. I think we're just going to go with the flow. We do have some surprises, but again, I don't want to talk about that. But yeah, it's going to be good. It's going to be an amazing experience.

**Travis:** Cool. One question I forgot to ask: do you have any favorite conferences that you've been to? And why?
