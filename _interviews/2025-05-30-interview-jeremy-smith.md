**Jeremy:** Yeah. The year before, I think 2022, was a really pivotal year for me professionally and in being involved in the Ruby and Rails community. Coming out of COVID, and after having spent 10 years working remotely from home, I was feeling pretty disconnected from people in general. I think a lot of people had this renewed sense of, I need to be around other people. I need to pursue some things that I suddenly couldn't pursue, and saw a need for in my own life.

So in 2022, I kind of went all out and went to a bunch of conferences. I work for myself. I've been doing that since 2013, and for many years it was hard to justify the cost of conferences a lot of times to myself, thinking, what am I going to get out of this that's going to make the anywhere from $1,000 to $3,000 that I throw down on a conference worth it?

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Jeremy:** And finally, in 2022, I was like, you know what, screw it. I want to do this. If this is my career, this is what I love doing, if I've been doing Ruby and Rails since 2009, and I love this and can't see myself leaving, why can't I just do this for myself? Because this is what I'm doing, maybe for the rest of my life.

I ended up going to several conferences. One was Sin City Ruby. Then I went to my first RailsConf in Portland, and then there was RubyConf Mini in Providence and Rails SaaS in LA. Three of those conferences were the first time those conferences had happened. I got to see the first time something had happened, and with all of this over the course of the year, it gave me a lot of context for what I liked or didn't like in conferences. I used to live there for a small period of time, and it's one of my favorite places I've ever spent time in. They have great food, beer, and music scenes. People come there for their weddings. <span id="delegating-is-its-own-job" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Jeremy:** That's what people don't understand. The thing everyone says is, that's too much work, you need to build a team. Either they're already good delegators and know how to build teams, or they don't understand that that is a job in itself.

**Travis:** Right.

**Jeremy:** It really is. I've spent most of my career working as a Rails developer solo or on very small teams, not usually running a team and doing almost everything myself. So I've optimized myself professionally to do things on my own. This was a venture into stepping out of that and not just doing the same thing. But in many ways, I don't have the skills to delegate well. I have not worked on those skills a lot. So this was a good experience of stepping more into that and trying to delegate.

One of the big challenges, even if you were really good at building a team and handing out responsibilities, is that I think that works really well when you know what the job is to do.

**Travis:** Right.

**Jeremy:** But if you've never done the job and you haven't seen it done well, how do you even know what needs to be done? I guess I would push back a little on that and just say, you do need people in your corner, and you do need people who are willing to do stuff. I know Adrian had a team, and they all split up the work and found a way to make that work. For me, it was hard to know what I could hand off and know: how will that have gone? Did that go? Will that have gone the way I needed to? Will I get the information back that I needed to make the next decision? That was really challenging.

I had a great volunteer team. I probably didn't delegate very well to them, and I think I could do that better a second time. But it's still hard when I'm also kind of a control freak and a perfectionist. The last thing I want to do is bring in people who are willing to give their time and attention and free time, and then be like, oh, that's not good enough, or I don't like what you did about that. That's really hard to figure out, too. How do you hit the quality level that you want, but not be a jerk to people that are just giving their free time and showing up when they didn't need to?

There are so many things about that that are really challenging, and I don't know exactly how to manage that. But the volunteers were a great team. We met up weekly and kind of talked about what we were doing. I think I did the bulk of the work for most things, but did hand off some things to them.

Having a team that had already spent time together before the event was really important, because the day of the event, you really do need multiple people in different areas. You need someone that's in charge of food and bev. You need someone that's in charge of registration. You need someone that's in charge of the speakers, making sure they have what they need. You need someone in charge of AV, all of that setup, making sure that's working well, and maybe even just venue or something. You can't do it all that day, and you don't want everyone just showing up and shaking hands for the first time, like, hey, hello, it's nice to meet you. Yes, you're in charge over here.

You really want to have spent time with those people before. So I do think it's important to build a team. I do think it's valuable if you can delegate, but especially you need people that day. I would recommend hanging out with them. Go get beer. Have a few meals together. Do some activities together, so that you know how to communicate well together and are ready. So that first day, you're not having difficulties communicating because you've never talked to each other, you don't know each other's personalities. If someone looks stressed, do they just always look that way, or are they communicating? You need to know what's going on with their personalities and how they communicate stuff.

**Travis:** Cool. Was there one thing that was hardest for you? Do you think there's one part of making a conference that's the hardest part?

**Jeremy:** The hardest part for me was dealing with people who were upset. I had a few situations like that. There weren't that many, but they stand out really clearly in my mind. I don't necessarily want to get into the details on that, but the thing about conferences is you have all these different types of people that have expectations on you. You make deals with your vendors, and they have expectations for you. Then you're selling tickets to attendees, so now they have expectations. You've invited speakers in. Now they have expectations. You've got some sponsorships. Now they have expectations.

Trying to manage all of those, and figure out what people's expectations are, and if they're reasonable, and then figuring out where you, if you fail at something, is really hard to manage. For me, I'm just anxious about that kind of stuff. I had a few situations where people got mad because they felt like I'd done something the wrong way.

**Travis:** A certain expectation, and you didn't know that or didn't meet that for some reason.

**Jeremy:** Yeah. In each situation, it did work out, but in the moment it was high anxiety for me. I found I'd really messed up and not knowing what to do with myself. That's again where having someone like Mark, who was pretty steady and like, you know what, we're going to be okay, that kind of person, not as fazed by some of those things, really helped. But for me, that can be a real emotional roller coaster.

**Travis:** Right.

**Jeremy:** I don't know that you can get around that. I think that's just the game. I could probably have prepared everything, and something else could have happened or would have happened. It feels inevitable.

I think maybe in the past I would have looked at those negative things, and it would have painted everything as, oh, I messed up those two or three things, it was terrible. The reality is, that's not true. There were two or three things that were really hard in the moment. They're not that big of a deal in the long term. The takeaway was over a hundred people had a really good time at this event. That's what I should take away, not those minor things. But that can be a hard thing, depending on your personality, how you handle those kind of things. I hate messing up and messing up with clients or messing up on projects, whatever it is. I just hate that. Especially when there's money involved, too. That makes it harder.

**Travis:** Yeah, I definitely get that. Definitely relate to that. When I met you at Sin City Ruby in 2023, I think—

I'd spoken at a conference here in Greenville one time, but never at a Ruby or Rails conference. So 2022 at RubyConf Mini was the first time I'd spoken at a Ruby event.

**Travis:** Right.

**Jeremy:** Then in 2023, starting to blur together.

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Jeremy:** What's the timeline of Sin City? I guess.

**Travis:** Was it like in March? And then was your first Blue Ridge in May?

**Jeremy:** Yeah. Sin City was. Yeah. I did speak in 2024 at Sin City. That was a talk that I gave at Friendly.rb.

**Travis:** 2024, right.

**Jeremy:** So yeah, starting to blur together. But I really did not have much network at all. It was something that I was actively working on improving, both through Twitter, but also by being involved at conferences and stuff like that. But I was starting with very little, I think.

Looking back now, that may have been somewhat of an anomaly, because I think one impact of COVID was that everyone came back and had a level of attention to community things that was not typical. It was like I just happened to partly hit that at the right time, just be in the right place at the right time, when lots of people were trying to get back and being really intentional about reconnecting. I was also thinking that, too. But I think I got lucky in some ways to make friends with a bunch of people that ended up really helping me along the way.

But running a conference is a good way of building a network, too. You play the host to a bunch of people that you look up to, or people that you know about, or people that have been in the community a long time, and that means that they all have an experience with you now that matters to them. It's not like I got to spend time with every single attendee, but I probably got to meet most of them.

That's another thing I noticed about Sin City especially, but also Rails SaaS. You have someone like Jason or someone like Andrew putting on an event, and now I have something that they've done for me that's really special. I now care about them as people even more than I did before, even more than the content that I got from them previously. Being able to show up to an event that's hosted, it's almost like a house party where you've been to somebody's house, and they served you and entertained you, and you had this really good experience because of them. That is kind of a valuable way of building a network. That helps.

**Travis:** How big was your first?

**Jeremy:** I think in total we had maybe 110, 120, something in that range. That included attendees, speakers, all the volunteers.

**Travis:** And how did you market? Did you have a marketing strategy? Was there anything that really worked well, and anything that was useless and you wouldn't do again? Anything that stood out there?

**Jeremy:** I think marketing is often underappreciated by developers especially, and I knew that going in. I had my work cut out for me there. I had to push that, both because I failed at building my own products around marketing a lot, but also because putting on something new like this, I needed to go from zero to 60 in five months. I needed to find a way to hit my numbers and sell tickets. How would people know if I didn't do that? They weren't just going to magically find out.

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Jeremy:** If I just trusted that people went to rubyconferences.org and were going to happen to see it, certainly some people, but that's a very small number. That's not going to be enough. So I was pulling out all the tricks that I knew. I've done marketing efforts. I used to build marketing landing pages. I used to help do marketing for various companies and clients that I had. So I was using whatever tactics I could think of, but I didn't know what would work for sure.

Partly I got lucky because of the timing that I mentioned. Suddenly there was a surge of interest, and I was able to ride that wave a little bit. I think there is a benefit to novelty. People like a new thing. People like seeing something new coming online. That is a big benefit.

But then also, I knew I needed to convey a level of quality through the stuff I put out as best I could. That meant spending a lot of time building the marketing site, doing a lot of social media stuff as much as I could think. I made a trailer video where I had my wife drive around Asheville. I was taking video with my phone in various places and put it together to make a video trailer, because I needed stuff. I needed to put things together and put things out there that would get people interested.

I built a city guide of all my favorite places to go or eat or drink, whatever it was. So people, when they came to town, would know what to experience. Partly there was a benefit of the destination. It's not just some random town somewhere that no one wants to hang out in, and you're just in a conference hall and then go back to your hotel. People want to come here and experience it. So I wanted to make sure it was able to check all the boxes on that for people, too, and that's a way to get people to come, because I know that happens. People wanted to visit, so it was even easier for them to buy a ticket.

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Jeremy:** We did some email as well. We definitely had an email list, and were sending out things that way. I might reach out more to other conferences. I've seen conferences do this, like conferences supporting each other. Hey, just want to highlight this.

Actually, I totally forgot about this. I think it was RailsConf. I went to a conference where I made these postcard mailers that looked like a mailer, that had all the details about Blue Ridge. I put a discount code on it, and then I got these custom stickers made with the logo, and I attached it with this little sticky thing so it kind of sat out in front of the card. Then people had something to put on their computer later, and people really seemed to like that. I would lay those out at tables. That must have been RailsConf. I'm forgetting what the conference was, but I was just pulling out all the tricks like that to try to get people to check it out or look at it.

As soon as, like with the other thing about trying to figure out things to share or things that entice people, one is the place they're going. What is the space going to be like? Before you even know who the speakers are, what am I going to experience in this place? It's figuring out: there are cool breweries in this town, there are really good restaurants, and the venue looks really nice. Then you do the CFP, and then you announce speakers, and then they're like, on top of that, I want to hear from this person, this person, this. I'd like the sound of that talk. So you're trying to hype that as well.

Sometimes people even do sharing the attendees that are going to be there as well. You're doing all those things to check all the boxes of the reasons people come to conferences, which is a combination of things. I want to experience a new place. I want to learn new things. I want to hear from these speakers or hear on these topics. I want to hang out with these kind of people. I want to do these kind of activities. People come for different reasons, and you're trying to show as many of those things as possible to entice people: you're going to get this and this. You're going to enjoy this and this as much as possible.

I don't know if I can pull off video, but I'm starting to put in these fixed costs, and then building a budget out of that. It was mostly luck. I think one had reached out to us, like, hey, we'd love to sponsor you. I thought, wow, that's super generous. I just met them a few months ago and would not have guessed that. **Travis:** Okay. Yeah.

**Jeremy:** He saw the company on the Stripe checkout page, not the product or whatever that you're selling. And he's like, is this actually—

<span id="selling-yourself-not-the-brand" class="interview-anchor"></span>

The bigger thing on the sponsorships is that it helps to have those personal connections. One of the best pieces of advice I got from another organizer, he said, especially when you're starting out, your brand, or the conference itself, is not what you're selling. You're selling you to sponsorships, particularly sponsors, but also attendees and speakers. You have to convince them that you are going to pull this off. Not that your Blue Ridge Ruby thing—they don't know that. They've never heard of it. You just made that up. It's you saying, I'm going to be on the hook to make sure that this event goes well. I'm going to be the one that's responsible. I'm the one that's looking you in the eye, saying I'm going to do everything in my power to make this a really great event that you're going to be happy with. What do you need from me? What can make this successful for you? They're making an agreement with you, and that really helped clarify for me the approach that I needed to take to win people's trust. That's what you're doing, building trust with everybody.

**Travis:** Right.

**Jeremy:** Speakers. Can they trust you? Attendees. Can they trust you? Vendors. Can they trust you? Sponsors. Can they trust you?

**Travis:** It's good advice, because I was thinking of it from the point of, how do I convince these people that this is going to be a conference worth sponsoring or worth attending, because I have no following. I have no experience in doing this, so everybody's taking a huge risk on you. But that does help. Instead of going to people and saying, look, I'm hoping to sell this many tickets, and it's going to be this kind of demographic, so you'll really get your money's worth because you're going to get in front of these people. It's probably better to sell yourself, because that's probably the more valuable thing in this situation. It's the only thing of value that you have really.

**Jeremy:** You don't have any hard numbers to show them. Here's what we did in the past. Here's what we're going to do. Here's our project.

**Travis:** Yeah.

**Jeremy:** It is people having to trust you. So you have to find sponsors, or hope that sponsors come to you, that will embrace that too. I don't think most sponsors are like that, for good reasons. They've got a certain amount of dollars they can spend, and they've got to place those bets on the conferences that are going to make the most sense for them.
