**Travis:** Gotcha. What was the hardest part, would you say, about organizing a conference?

**Spike:** Well, I'll speak specifically to 2023, and then in general. In 2023, my problem was a venue. Rocky Mountain Ruby had traditionally been in a place called Boulder Theater, which is an old movie theater that's been converted to a conference venue. Back when it was previously running, they were very happy to have us in there, because it fills up the theater during the day, and we could be there till 5 or 6, and it's almost always available.

When I talked to them this time, they're like, sorry, bands load in at 2 o'clock now. Because concerts started post-COVID, concerts start at 8, everybody goes home by 10, so we don't have it unless you want to be out by noon. We can't do it. Then there was a big scramble to find someplace that would take us. We were during the school year, so we couldn't use the university. Hotels are very expensive. There aren't a lot of other venues in Boulder that are about the size we were looking for, so that was very difficult.

Then we landed on eTown Hall, and that turned out to be perfect. I hadn't even considered them, because somebody told me they were just super expensive, which turned out not to be true. They've been great to work with. We could talk about choosing a venue, because I think there are a lot of choices there and a lot of trade-offs.

But generally speaking, the way I like to describe it is: it's not difficult, but it is hard. What you have to do is a Kanban board, right? You need to do this, and then this, and then this, and make sure you've done that. It's straightforward. It's easy enough to put it into a checklist or a board or something, but it's the amount of work that's hard. There's a lot of nights and weekends and just getting stuff. There's always a deadline coming up, and then as you get closer, there's a lot of wrangling and last-minute stuff and changes. That's the thing I think is hard about running conferences: the time commitment.

**Travis:** Gotcha. So on the business side of it, I'm assuming that maybe Rocky Mountain Ruby already had some sort of business organization or entity, or didn't?

<span id="setting-up-an-llc" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Spike:** No. Whatever had existed had gone away, so I created it. There are obviously different ways you can do it. My recommendation is set up an LLC and have that run the conference. It just keeps all the finances separate from whatever else. I think back when Marty ran it, he actually ran it under his consulting business. But I think if it's just a distinct entity, then with its own bank account, all the money is in its own place. It's just simpler for taxes and all that.

We are not currently a nonprofit, because of just a ton of paperwork. That's something we'd like to do in the future and something that Ruby Central has talked about being able to help with, because I know the Python Foundation helps with that, just filing all the paperwork that you have to do to be a nonprofit for conferences, user groups, and things like that. So that's something we'd like to do. In Colorado, I think it was 10 bucks to do an LLC. Then I opened a bank account. I basically lent money to the LLC to open a bank account, and with that, then I could start paying people. I could get a Stripe account for the tickets and for getting money from sponsors, and just ran it that way.

**Travis:** Does becoming a nonprofit simplify or help the taxes? Most of the time, you're not making a lot of money on conferences anyway, so you might as well be a nonprofit. Is that kind of why you're moving?

**Spike:** Yeah. If you have money left over, you have to pay taxes on it. I don't mind doing that, except it just seems like a waste. It's money we could put back in next year, or money we could donate to Ruby Central or the Boulder Ruby group or something like that, instead of just paying it on taxes. So that's the main reason. It does make it easier for people to give us money as well. If somebody wanted to donate, they really can't donate to an LLC. It's not tax deductible for them. So there are some advantages to it. But at the moment, it's not worth the headache. You basically need an accountant who specializes in nonprofits at that point.

**Travis:** Gotcha. Okay. How did you, assuming you had one, determine the budget for your first one?

**Spike:** Very kind of back of the napkin. I just had a big spreadsheet and kept tweaking the ticket prices and guessing at how many I would sell and that kind of thing.

Let me back up a little bit and talk about picking the venue and the process, because that kind of leads to everything financially. First, I needed to find a date. This is something that you really need to do carefully, because that first year we overlapped with a marathon, and there was an Ironman or something, and it drove up the hotel prices. I didn't know that.

Boulder is where the University of Colorado is. Their football program is kind of popular, and a lot more popular now that they have Deion Sanders as a coach. You cannot get a hotel room if they're playing a game, so I can't pick those weekends. Boulder is a big outdoor activity town, so you have to avoid things like Ironmans. Finding a weekend where there's nothing big going on—and because we're at the end of September, beginning of October period, there's also the Jewish High Holidays, and I don't want to have my conference on those days and then have people not be able to come because of that. So you've got to nail a date, and then you've got to find a venue. That's going to be your biggest expense. So what I did before I started dialing in the budget was: all right, where can we be?

<span id="budgeting-stretch-goals" class="interview-anchor"></span>

Then for the first year, and I got this idea from Jeremy Smith, we went with sort of stretch goals. If we sell this many tickets, we will have t-shirts. If we sell this many tickets, we will have breakfast. If we really go all the way and sell all the tickets, we will record the conference and post the videos. That was a good way to not overextend.

It was still a bit of a nail-biter at the end, because I really wanted to do the videos. I think that is super valuable to the people who are speaking, because now, when they want to speak at a bigger conference or get a job, they can point to this video. And it's also great promotion for future years. So that was important to me. But that was tough and really came down to some last-minute sponsorship we got, and ConFreaks, who we use and I highly recommend, being willing to work on a payment plan with us.

**Travis:** Right. I have no idea what prices are. I haven't dug into a lot of this stuff down to, like, I need to look into what venues in Albuquerque are like. That's where I am planning on having it right now.

**Spike:** I was guessing from the big map behind you.

**Travis:** Yeah. But what is general for recording? Is that to the tune of thousands of dollars? Do you have to buy them out?

**Spike:** Yes. I'm speaking specifically of ConFreaks, who I highly recommend. They take care of travel and hotel, but you have to pay for their time. I would say they're the second biggest expense behind venue.

**Travis:** Okay.

**Spike:** But I think it's worth it. For example, one conference I won't mention hired a wedding photographer, friend of a friend, seemed like a great deal. But recording a conference is difficult, right? You need to get the speakers and their audio. And you need to get the slides. Without the slides, most talks are just useless. They didn't know how to capture the slides, and the audio wasn't great, and it took six months to get the videos out, because basically what they had to do was ask everybody for their slide decks and then manually create a video of the slide deck to go with it.

If you hire somebody who knows how to do this, it goes a lot smoother. In the case of ConFreaks, Cindy is the owner. She actually did a lightning talk at Rocky Mountain Ruby. She did a talk at the last Ruby conference, because her brother started this, and they've been involved in the Ruby community forever. They just know what they're doing. They record the video. You give them logos. You give them all the sponsor information. Then boom, videos turn up on YouTube. It's one less thing to think about.

I specifically recommend ConFreaks, but I definitely recommend working with somebody who's done this before. And if you take a hotel as a venue, they'll often have somebody who does this.

So that was how I handled the finances of the first year. The second year was a little easier, because I had a sense of how many tickets we would sell. One thing that's really weird with selling tickets is, you would think people would buy tickets in advance, but they really don't. The first year we cut off ticket sales like a week out, because we just didn't want to deal with, oh no, we need more badges, we need more t-shirts, whatever. People started complaining. I ended up selling like 30-odd tickets in the last week. That was like 20% of the ticket sales happened a week before the conference.

**Travis:** Those are probably locals.

**Spike:** Yeah, that's exactly what I was going to say. If you're RailsConf or RubyConf, you probably don't have this problem, because there's far less locals. But I would say half to two-thirds of our people are local. They just wait until the last minute, see if their boss will let them go, or whatever, that kind of thing.

So that was kind of surprising. But the second year was a little easier because I knew to expect that. By then, I had dialed in all the catering and everything, so I knew what everything was going to cost, plus or minus inflation. It was much easier to get sponsors the second year because we could point to the videos, everything that had gone well with the previous year. So I had more sponsors in 2024. I think we'll do fine in 2025 with sponsorship. One sponsor can pay for the videos or the venue or whatever.

**Travis:** How did you go about getting sponsors the first year? Was it pretty much just people in your network, or how did you go about that?

**Spike:** Yeah, it was people in our network. I reached out to people I know at big companies who I thought might be able to come up with some money. Looking at my sponsor list from that year, I knew people at Gusto and FusionAuth, and they gave us some money. That was kind of enough that year.

Also, when it comes to who's listed as a sponsor: anyone who paid for their own travel. We offer a little stipend to speakers, $500 to help with their travel. We can't afford, we're not a conference that can afford to fly people out, but we offer that. People who turned it down, we would then put their company as a sponsor. Basically, the company is paying for travel.

**Travis:** Okay. Cool.

**Spike:** If you look at 2024, we had Podia and a few others, and it was just because they sent their own people out. Then in 2024, I think all of our sponsors reached out to me. Once we announced we opened the CFP, they're like, hey, can we sponsor you? They were all people—I think two of the three, and I can't remember who was who now—but the big sponsors were Honeybadger, AppSignal, and DNSimple. Two of the founders had actually spoken at Rocky Mountain Ruby in the past, and the third had attended a bunch of times. So they had that connection. That was definitely nice.

**Travis:** Yeah. From a timeline perspective, or an order, maybe, I'm trying to start a year out. I am shooting for something in May or June of next year. I was thinking probably doing venue and sponsors first. Sponsors for me specifically is going to be really hard because I don't have a wide network. I guess I was thinking maybe start that earlier because it's going to be hard. But at the same time, it's going to be even harder because I don't know people, and I'm reaching out, and I'm saying like, hey, this is my first conference. Never done this before. No idea how this is going to go. Do you want to give me money? Were you getting sponsors later in the process?

**Spike:** It was a little later in the process. I started trying pretty much as soon as I had the website and the sponsor sheet up, and we should talk about that in a second. But it did take a little while and a little poking.

One of the things that Ruby Central wants to do post-RailsConf is help, and they're already doing this with meetups where they're connecting meetups with potential sponsors. The Boulder Ruby group has had a pizza sponsor for the last few months through Ruby Central. So I think talking to Ali and all, they can get you in front of people. Now, will they give you money? That's a harder thing. But one of the things Ruby Central is really trying to do, especially with the bigger Rails shops, is like, hey, we're not doing RailsConf anymore, but we can get you in front of 30 Ruby groups, 50 Ruby groups, and 10 conferences. So work together on that.

I think that's working that network and not necessarily going directly to the sponsors beyond Ruby Central. But if you're friends with Jason Swett, who can he connect you with? Because that personal connection, I think, works better than just reaching out cold to Cisco or something like that.

In terms of timeline, pick a date, get a venue, because venues are the thing you have to book out the furthest. Build a website and put up your sponsor sheet. I just copied mine from Blue Ridge Ruby. You could copy mine, you could copy somebody else's, and be flexible on it.

We went into the jewel theme because Rocky Mountain has emerald at $2,000, sapphire at $4,000, and ruby at $5,000. Everybody gets their logo on the site, they get recognition, and then it's like, well, where are you on the page? How many complimentary tickets does your organization get? Do you get a table or not? At the top tier, you get a table to yourself. At the middle tier, you get to share one. At the bottom tier, you can just put your stickers wherever.

But we were not, and I don't recommend being, particularly hard-ass with this. Be flexible. What do they actually want? Maybe if they're not hiring, they might not want a table. They might just want to put their stickers out. They might want a little bump up. They might want an in-between amount of money because that's what their budget is. Fine. If you can give me $3,000, I'll give you the sapphire thing.

**Travis:** You want them to sponsor you. Ultimately, aside from if you sell out, it's not costing you that much to do this.

**Spike:** So work with them. We've talked to sponsors—nobody's actually taken us up on this—but other things like host a game night, right? That'd be great. Or sponsor an after-party or organize a hike. I'm happy to put you on the website, because that stuff around the conference is also very helpful.

Once you have a site and you have a sponsor sheet, that's when I started reaching out to people. It does get easier to do things, of course, once you have some talks up. People do different things. Some folks put their tickets on sale as soon as they have a date. We generally have waited until we've picked the speakers, because then we can say, this is what you're paying for. This is why you want to come. Aside from how cool we are, here are the actual talks.

This year, I think we're going to have a keynote that we can pre-announce before the CFP closes. We're still talking about that. But we've gotten to the point where we can probably have an invited keynote, and that is something that's enough of a splash, I think, for us to start selling tickets. But if you're comfortable selling them from the beginning and think people want to come to Albuquerque, even, you can do that too. I don't think there's a right answer there.

My thought the first few years, anyway, was, we've been gone for five years or so. We're not RubyConf or RailsConf. People aren't just going to automatically buy tickets. So now that we've done this a few years, we have a reputation. I think putting tickets on sale before we have the full program built out is okay.

**Travis:** What did you mean when you said you've reached the point where you can have an invited keynote?

**Spike:** Just that now we have enough of a reputation that people are always telling us they want to come.

I mean, we could always do that, right? But in the past years—

**Travis:** Did you invite people in the past years?

**Spike:** No. Well, we tried to and couldn't find anyone who would come. Again, we were new and people didn't know what to expect.

We'll talk about scheduling. The biggest mistake we made the first year was not the marathon. I had forgotten this part, but Rails World was the week before.

**Travis:** Oh, yeah.

**Spike:** It was the beginning of the same week, actually. And then Thai Ruby was the same days that we were. So everyone I reached out to was either at Rails World or Thai Ruby. Why wouldn't you be?

So this year we're not near Thai Ruby; it moved, changed the time of year, and Rails World is a couple of weeks before. Last year, for whatever reason, we didn't really try. This year, I think we're going to reach out to some names in the community and see if anybody just wants to come, and then we can do that. But there's no reason not to do that your first year if you have the connections. If there's somebody you think would be a draw, or somebody you know gives a good talk, then no reason not to ask them. Put them on the program and have something you can post to social media.

**Travis:** Did you, for all speakers, offer that stipend? Everybody, same stipend?

**Spike:** Yeah.

**Travis:** Gotcha.

**Spike:** You don't have to. Rocky Mountain Ruby didn't in the past. There are different philosophies. I know when Marty ran it, he never did. Just, sorry, we can't afford that. Then there are other conferences that pay the full thing. We looked at those numbers, and there is no way we could make that work. So we just settled on the middle, and we're going to review that again this year.

Most people don't take it, though. So I think it's a nice thing to offer to people who are coming great distances and juniors who may not be able to afford it on their own. But we make it clear: we do not have a lot of money, so if you don't need it, please, we'll give you sponsor credit. But yeah, it's something I might consider not doing your first year, just because it is really hard to budget for.

**Travis:** That makes sense. When you were marketing the conference, was there anything that you did that unexpectedly got you a bunch of people buying tickets because you did this one thing?

<span id="marketing-linkedin-podcasts" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Spike:** A couple of things worked well. We did a lot of social media stuff and a lot of LinkedIn. LinkedIn, I think, is the best place these days because social media is in a weird state. We still have a Twitter account. I don't know how we feel about it. We have a Bluesky account, but I think most of our interactions come through LinkedIn.

One of the things where I noticed a big bump of ticket sales was LinkedIn. Whatever their algorithm is, it seems pretty localized, like who sees your message. It's in your network, but people outside of your network, I think it's more like your town. We got a big bump when I got some of my friends who are in Denver to post stuff, because I think then it got shown to more people in Denver. So that was pretty effective. Think about getting folks who are in places that people might drive to your conference from to share it. Not just reshare, but actually share it. That seems to do something in the LinkedIn algorithm.

The other thing I actually had really good luck with was podcasts. We reached out to a bunch of podcasts. I had a friend who does some podcast editing put together a 30-second clip for me, and then I sent it out to Ruby on Rails Podcast, Code and the Coding Coders Who Code It with Drew Bragg, The Bike Shed, and Remote Ruby. Just having them play that I think definitely helps. They're all happy to do it.

**Travis:** So that was like an ad spot?

**Spike:** Yeah, an ad spot, and I offered to pay, and none of them made me. But that's something you need to do way in advance, because people's editorial calendars tend to be booked out.

**Travis:** Okay. So they're looking months out.

**Spike:** Exactly.

**Travis:** Gotcha. Okay.

**Spike:** Going on podcasts: the first year I went on Ruby on Rails Podcast with Brittany Martin. And you heard us on Remote Ruby. We're going to be on Ode to RailsConf next week. So just getting out there.

Now, of course, we have the Ruby Conferences website, where you can just make a pull request and put your conference in rubyconferences.org. The Ruby Central Slack is very active now, so that's a great place to just promote stuff. Then there's also Ruby Central Discord. It's less popular since they rolled out their Slack, but that's also a good place to start looking for people.

**Travis:** Was there anything that you did when you were marketing that was just a waste of time? Like, don't do it?

**Spike:** Not really, only because we didn't try much because we didn't have a budget. We looked at Facebook ads and Twitter ads and things like that, and just decided it wasn't worth it.

**Travis:** Gotcha. Okay. Do you go to a lot of conferences yourself?

**Spike:** Not really. I've been going to more. Since 2022, I've gone to most of the RubyConf and RailsConf events. I'll be going to RailsConf in July. I hope to go to RubyConf. In the past, I always went to Rocky Mountain Ruby. And then there was MountainWest Ruby in Salt Lake City, so I'd go to at least two a year.

I used to be not good at conferences, like many of us: kind of an introvert and shy, and those kinds of things. At some point in my life, I stopped caring what people think about me. They're going to like me, or they won't, and it turns out most of the time they do. So I got better at just like, hey, I'll just go talk to this random person and see what they're like.

**Travis:** Definitely. Is there anything that you do at your conferences to make it a better experience for people? Do you think that the main thing for conferences is the social aspect? Because a lot of these talks could be a blog post or a YouTube video, right? It's nice to see them in person. But do you think that socialization is the main point of conferences?

**Spike:** I do, and you need talks, because otherwise why would your boss let you go?

**Travis:** Right, yeah.

**Spike:** I do learn stuff, right? To your point, talks are often better in person, and I learn stuff even at my own conference. My big takeaway from 2024 was Packwerk. I hadn't learned anything about that framework. But as I work on bigger projects, having that kind of thing—and you get different perspectives, and that's always handy.

We try to mix, because we're not a Rails conference. We try to mix in other frameworks and things like that. So I learned a lot about Hanami, and I may never use it, but just knowing that there are different ways that people do things and think about things is super helpful.

The advantage of a small conference like Rocky Mountain Ruby is we can do both. We do a total of 16 talks, just eight talks a day. We do two talks, a half-hour break, two talks, half-hour break. So there's lots of time. The venue is really nice because it's a concert venue, so the big concert hall, but then downstairs there's a bar area where people can just hang and talk to each other.

Colorado in October is one of our best months weather-wise. Probably similar to New Mexico: just not too hot, it's sunny, it doesn't rain. People will go hang out outside.

The other thing, and I totally stole this from Blue Ridge, and I think they stole it from Brighton Ruby, is we just do open lunch. We have a two-hour block. Just go out in the world and have lunch. We have a list of restaurants. Boulder is a big foodie town, so it's super easy for people to just go out. We actively help try to find people groups. That's really great for the social aspect. It's also great for the budget, because catering lunch is expensive and usually not great.

That is a big part of what we do. Then in the afternoon, we again continue to have big breaks. That's easier to do in a small conference when it's single track. You're going to see all the talks. I think there's something to people in a single-track conference all seeing the same talks. They all have the same frame of reference when they're talking in between, whereas at RubyConf or RailsConf, where there's three or five tracks, you're more scattered if you're going to all the talks.

I do think that RubyConf and RailsConf, the hack day they have now has really helped with that social aspect. I think they're calling it community now, but having a day where it's less structured and people can just hang out works well. So there's lots of different ways to do it.

That's what we landed on, and it works well. Then there's just stuff we can do to encourage it. My co-organizer, Becky, came back from Rails World with the Pac-Man idea: whenever you're in a circle, open up a little wedge so that there's always room for someone else to join the conversation. So we promote that kind of, hey, talk to your neighbors. Talk to the juniors. If you see somebody who's clearly just standing on the sidelines, try talking to them, and be respectful if they just don't want to talk. That's cool. But usually you can engage with somebody, and they'll want to talk.

We really push that because, to your point, I think there's value out of the tech stuff you learn, but you're going to make connections that are going to be useful to have in your career, or are going to be fun. I now know all these people from doing this I didn't know before, like Chris Oliver and Drew Bragg and Nadia. My circle has greatly expanded, and these are people I can reach out to and ask when I have a question, or help me with a job search or help them. It's really nice to have a community.

**Travis:** Yeah, definitely. Alright. Well, we're coming up on time. I wanted to be respectful of your time. But do you have any other advice that you can think of for a first-time organizer?

<span id="recruiting-volunteers" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Spike:** Yes. My biggest advice is: get all the volunteers you can. I could not have done it without them. I'm fortunate that in Boulder, we have a good Ruby meetup that's been going for 20 years now, I think. We have a Slack where everyone just kind of hangs out. So I was able to reach out and say, hey, anyone want to volunteer? I'll give you a ticket, basically.

Very quickly out of that, I got someone who's good at social media, and she's still doing that for us. I got someone who's willing to tackle the catering, because we do cater breakfast, so you've got to find somebody to take care of all that. Then there was Becky, who volunteered to help with swag.

We learned a lot about swag. This is another quick advice: you have to order it way earlier than you think. All those places are like, hey, we can turn t-shirts around in two days. That's only if they're not busy, and they're always busy. So it turns out to be like a month. That's one of the scariest things: how many people are actually coming, and what size t-shirts do they need? What we do is actually cut it off and say, two weeks out, we're not going to guarantee you a t-shirt, or you can pay more and we'll order a t-shirt for you after. You have a whole chart of how many people are wearing what, what are the likely sizes that we need, that kind of stuff.

Becky volunteered to do that: shirts, stickers, badges. And she just kept doing other things. So at the end of it, I'm like, look, you've done so much work.
