<span id="skipping-invites-for-cfp-only" class="interview-anchor"></span>

**Hana:** Yeah, that was really interesting, because we did plan to invite keynote speakers. But we also wanted to already open the CFP so that people have time. And then we got so many great proposals that we realized we actually don't need to invite more people or specific people, because we were very happy with the selection which we did.

So we ended up, it was a plan to have keynote speakers, or to invite keynote speakers, and then we found out that we actually don't need it. And that was really nice. So we kept that, and we always did just the CFP without direct invitations so far. But it could change. If we ever say, oh, we really want to invite this person, and we feel up to that, then we still can. It's more like we didn't manage to do this before the CFP, and then we didn't need it.

**Travis:** That's really cool. That's another simplification that I hadn't thought of, really, where if you're especially in your first conference, you're just trying to do things as simply as possible to take the pressure and the stress off. Just doing a CFP instead of trying to do outreach to a bunch of speakers for keynote actually simplifies it a little bit, and might be a good route to go, because the Ruby community has a lot of interest in speaking, and CFPs are usually very, very successful. So that's cool. And you guys have done that every year?

**Hana:** Yes, yes, we do that every year. When opening the CFP, it was always important for us to reach different groups of people, so we made sure that we announced the CFP also on the main channels, like the social channels, but also support or motivate people from underrepresented groups, or voices which are not always heard that much, that they also submit talks. We wanted to have a different range, so from seasoned speakers, we also wanted to have people who are just starting.

At the beginning, we put together a list of what criteria our selection — not every talk, but the final selection — should look like, what we would like to see, and we try to fulfill all of them. It's not like we have to do checkmarks. If the selection works well, and even if it doesn't fulfill all of our wishes, then it's fine. I think every year it was like that, but we always had that in mind up front, and it was important for us to have a wide CFP. We never actually ran into the problem that we would have to adjust our selection based on the criteria.

It was such a surprise, especially the first year, because I was fearing this moment a lot, like, okay, selecting the lineup. It's three of us, we have different opinions, what will we do? I expected it will be a lengthy process with a lot of discussions. It ended up working out really well. Everyone did their pre-selection of the talks. It was three of us, and then we compared. There were a bunch of talks which all of us would like to see, and then there were others which had two people as a want to see and another person as, yeah, this is okay, and then we just put them together.

Then we looked at our criteria, and we were like, oh, this is fulfilled, this is fulfilled too, this too. So we ended up having a great lineup without a lot of discussion, fulfilling what we wished for, so that was surprisingly easy, and I was very happy about that. But it's not always that simple. I think that was also because of the quality of the proposals, and that we discussed up front how we want the lineup to look, or what are the talks we are looking for.

That happened a bunch of times, that the catering, either they forgot, or they misunderstood, or they wrote something else, or there were no people. But because we were kind of ready that these things may happen, the attendees never realized that. That's really like, planning helps a lot.

**Hana:** Yeah, so we try to just prepare everything up front. What do you think makes a good conference? Is there anything that you try to focus on, because that's what you think makes a good conference?

**Hana:** I think, again, it's a mix of things. First, we really want that people feel welcome and safe, and that the talks are for a wide range of audience. Either it can be the topic itself, or it can be a talk which is structured so that the beginning is accessible, or it makes sense for all levels, also juniors and mid-career levels, and then at the end there may be more of a deep dive. Networking was always important for us, so we actually have breaks after every talk. Those breaks are usually at least 20 minutes. So this gives opportunity for people first to ask follow-up questions to the speakers, but also catch up with other attendees, take a break, and come back refreshed for another talk. We also wanted to have a balanced lineup, and that the talks are about different topics for different audiences from different people.

**Travis:** Gotcha. So it's like they're not all technical talks. You have some programming philosophy, or career advice, those kind of different categories.

**Hana:** Yes, we usually aim for, again, always depending on the proposals, having two non-technical talks and five technical talks.
